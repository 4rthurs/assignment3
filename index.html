People influencing how I think,

David Senra

Breaks down how great founders actually think and operate over long periods of time, not just during big moments. A lot of focus on obsession, patience, and committing fully to one thing instead of spreading attention. He consistently reinforces that building something meaningful takes years of repetition and focus. It removes the illusion of overnight success and replaces it with discipline. Listening to him shifts your mindset toward long-term thinking and doing fewer things better.

@davidsenra

Blaze Smith

Pushes a raw, unconventional approach to design through Shovel Studio. The work feels intentional but not overpolished, which gives it a stronger identity. There's a clear focus on direction and taste rather than following trends or over-explaining ideas. It shows that design doesn't need to be perfect to be effective. More than anything, it proves that having a point of view matters more than trying to please everyone.

@shovel.studio

Dan Alves

Focuses on building brands with clarity, strong positioning, and purpose behind every decision. His transition from Mokk to Comunal shows a willingness to adapt and rethink direction instead of staying stuck. There's a clear emphasis on execution, not just ideas or aesthetics. It highlights how branding, product, and strategy all need to align. His approach reinforces that building something real means constantly refining and improving.

@dalves

Frank Niu

Keeps things direct and practical when it comes to money, careers, and decision-making. Focuses heavily on playing the long game instead of chasing short-term wins or trends. He simplifies complex topics into clear, actionable ideas without overcomplicating them. There's a strong emphasis on using time well and making intentional choices. His perspective pushes you to think more critically about how you approach work and opportunities.

What I've learned so far,

Arthur Santos

I'm drawn to things that feel intentional, whether it's in fashion, design, or how something is built. Most of what I like comes down to strong direction, simplicity, and knowing what to leave out. I pay attention to how ideas translate into real execution, not just how they look. A lot of my perspective comes from studying brands, creators, and how they move over time. This archive is a way to track that and make sense of what actually sticks.

Things I've learned lately

We look to maintain ongoing relationships.

Taste > Trends

Trends move fast and are easy to copy, which makes most things feel the same. Strong taste takes longer to build but gives your work a clear identity. Over time, taste compounds and becomes way more valuable than chasing what's popular.

Execution beats ideas

Ideas don't mean much without action behind them. A simple idea executed well will always beat a great idea that never gets built. Finishing things consistently is what actually moves you forward.

Less, but better

Most work gets worse when you keep adding to it. Cutting things down forces clarity and makes what's left stronger. Simplicity isn't basic—it's usually harder to get right.

Consistency wins long-term

One good result doesn't matter if you can't repeat it. Showing up and doing the work over time builds real progress. Consistency compounds, even when it feels slow.

Direction > Perfection

Having a clear point of view matters more than making everything perfect. Work with strong direction stands out, even if it's a bit rough. Trying to please everyone usually weakens the result.

Attention is earned

People don't care unless something actually grabs them. Good work makes someone stop, even for a second. If it blends in, it might as well not exist.
